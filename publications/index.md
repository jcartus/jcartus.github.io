			<p class="publication-meta">The Journal of Physical Chemistry C, 2024</p>
			<h2>Kinetic Trapping of Charge-Transfer Molecules at Metal Interfaces</h2>
			<p class="publication-authors">Anna Werkovits, Simon B. Hollweger, Max Niederreiter, Thomas Risse, Johannes J. Cartus, Martin Sterrer, Sebastian Matera, and Oliver T. Hofmann</p>
			<p>Despite the common expectation that conjugated organic molecules on metals adsorb in a flat-lying layer, thermodynamically stable upright-standing phases may be more common than experiments suggest. Using first-principles kinetic Monte Carlo simulations together with infrared and X-ray photoemission spectroscopy for TCNE on Cu(111), this work shows that the lower-density flat phase usually forms first and can remain kinetically trapped under common surface-science growth conditions.</p>
			<p class="publication-links"><a href="https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.jpcc.3c08262">DOI: 10.1021/acs.jpcc.3c08262</a></p>

			<h2>Interplay of Adsorption Geometry and Work Function Evolution at the TCNE/Cu(111) Interface</h2>
			<p class="publication-authors">Max Niederreiter, Johannes Cartus, Anna Werkovits, Oliver T. Hofmann, Thomas Risse, and Martin Sterrer</p>
			<p>This work combines XPS, IRRAS, and density functional theory to link adsorption geometry directly to work-function evolution for TCNE on Cu(111). It shows that the monolayer forms mixed face-on and edge-on structures whose distribution changes with coverage, and that the nearly linear work-function increase up to about 2.9 eV is primarily driven by dipoles of free nitrile groups in edge-on molecules.</p>
			<p class="publication-links"><a href="https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.jpcc.3c06422">DOI: 10.1021/acs.jpcc.3c06422</a> | <a href="https://pubs.acs.org/doi/full/10.1021/acs.jpcc.3c06422">ACS</a></p>

			<h2>Impact of Static Distortion Waves on Superlubricity</h2>
			<p class="publication-authors">Lukas Hörmann, Johannes J. Cartus, and Oliver T. Hofmann</p>
			<p>Friction in incommensurate interfaces is often assumed to vanish, but that picture breaks down once mesoscale structural relaxations are resolved. By combining density functional theory with a machine-learned interatomic potential, this work shows how static distortion waves can strongly increase friction and identifies design principles for organic/metal interfaces in which ultralow friction is preserved.</p>
			<p class="publication-links"><a href="https://doi.org/10.1021/acsomega.3c05044">DOI: 10.1021/acsomega.3c05044</a></p>

			<p class="publication-meta">Nanoscale Advances, 2023</p>
			<h2>Polymorphism mediated by electric fields: a first principles study on organic/inorganic interfaces</h2>
			<p class="publication-authors">Johannes J. Cartus, Andreas Jeindl, Anna Werkovits, Lukas Hörmann, and Oliver T. Hofmann</p>
			<p>Organic/inorganic interfaces often show multiple polymorphs with strongly different properties, but many of them are difficult to access using temperature and pressure alone. Using TCNE on Cu(111) as a model system, this study demonstrates that external electric fields reshape molecule-substrate and molecule-molecule interactions strongly enough to shift the lying-standing phase boundary by about 100 K.</p>
			<p class="publication-links"><a href="https://doi.org/10.1039/D2NA00851C">DOI: 10.1039/D2NA00851C</a></p>

			<p class="publication-authors">Anna Werkovits, Andreas Jeindl, Lukas Hörmann, Johannes J. Cartus, and Oliver T. Hofmann</p>
			<p>Focusing on TCNE on Cu(111), this study combines density functional theory, nudged elastic band calculations, and transition state theory to quantify diffusion and reorientation barriers during growth. It identifies a practical temperature window where flat-lying molecules can still diffuse to form ordered layers while the thermodynamically preferred reorientation into upright structures remains kinetically suppressed.</p>

The interfaces remain weakly physisorbed and largely preserve the molecular frontier orbitals, while hybridization with delocalized Au states produces dispersive interfacial bands that point toward low charge-injection barriers at organic-metal contacts.</p>
